## Tone & Emotional Core

> *"Drifting in the lonely but magnificent deep space, while reflecting on the meaning of it all."*

This is the emotional spine of the entire game. The universe is vast, sparse, mostly unknown, and achingly beautiful. The player is a conscious, small being inside something incomprehensible. Every faction, NPC, named system, and piece of dialogue should serve this tone.

This is not a universe of heroes and villains. It is a universe of beings trying to find meaning in the same enormous dark.

### Canonical Tone

The setting combines **political realism, cosmic horror, and human connection**.

- Political institutions act from material needs, internal pressures, incomplete
  information, and competing ideas of survival. No faction is wholly correct or
  reducible to a moral alignment.
- Cosmic horror comes primarily from the universe's indifference, from
  intelligences capable of understanding conscious beings completely, and from
  the discovery that reality follows principles ordinary consciousness cannot
  safely perceive.
- Personal connection remains meaningful precisely because the universe does
  not guarantee that meaning. Care, loyalty, hospitality, memory, and shared
  work are local acts rather than cosmic answers.

Humanity is not central to the setting and may not be identifiable as a
distinct historical or present civilization. The player's species and the
humanity of apparently familiar people remain deliberately ambiguous unless a
future canon decision requires otherwise.

### Epistemic Boundary

The lore distinguishes between **authorial truth**, **in-world belief**, and
**unresolved truth**. Not every central mystery has a canonical answer.

Whether a consciousness survives ascension is permanently unresolved. The
possibilities include continuity, replacement by a copy, transformation beyond
the usefulness of identity, or an outcome lower-tier minds cannot represent.
No faction, character, codex entry, simulation result, or Tier 4 communication
may settle the question conclusively.

Evidence may support incompatible interpretations. Encounters can make one
interpretation feel persuasive, but later evidence may disturb it without
turning the mystery into a puzzle with a final solution. The Company of Doom,
the Concordance, the Truth Seekers, Tier 3 entities, and Tier 4 entities can all
hold or imply different positions; none speaks with authorial authority.

## Universe Macro-Structure

| Property | State |
|---|---|
| **Scale** | Procedurally vast. Most of the universe is genuinely unknown, even to the most advanced Tier 2 civilizations. |
| **Exploration status** | Sparse and half-explored. Huge regions between known systems have never been visited by any recorded civilization. |
| **Faction age** | All Tier 2 factions emerged roughly in parallel. No dominant empire. No historical winner. Peers in the dark. |
| **World state** | Fully emergent. No authored current events — the simulation generates them. |

### The Living Rarity and the Dead Multitude

The six Tier 2 factions are genuinely separate civilizations. They did not
descend from a shared culture, species, polity, or known precursor. Each
developed FTL independently and encountered the others only after reaching
interstellar space.

Living technological civilizations are extraordinarily rare across both
distance and any single historical period. The six contemporaries are therefore
an exceptional convergence, not evidence of a crowded universe.

Extinct civilizations, however, are numerous in the aggregate. The universe is
old enough that sparse life has left many layers of ruins, signals, altered
worlds, abandoned machines, and disputed traces. Most civilizations discovered
by the living factions are known only through remains. Distance, incomplete
records, incompatible minds, and deep time make it difficult to determine why
they disappeared or whether they truly ceased to exist.

This distinction is foundational: **life is rare, but the dead are many**.
Exploration repeatedly confronts living beings with evidence that they are not
the first to cross the dark, while offering no reliable assurance that they
will avoid the same silence.

### The FTL Convergence

The near-contemporary emergence of six unrelated FTL civilizations is an
unresolved anomaly. It is too improbable to dismiss comfortably, but there is
no authoritative explanation for it. It may reflect an unknown natural
threshold, selection, synchronization, preparation by another intelligence, or
an error in how lower-tier civilizations understand chronology and causation.
The canon does not choose among these explanations.

This convergence is not necessarily common public knowledge in every culture.
Its evidence can be incomplete, politically controlled, disputed, or dangerous
to investigate. No discovered archive, extinct civilization, Tier 3 entity, or
Tier 4 communication may provide a conclusive solution.

There was no single shared first-contact era. Contact happened unevenly:

- Some faction pairs have known one another for generations and possess mature
  diplomatic customs, inherited grievances, mixed communities, and established
  trade.
- Some relationships are recent, with translation, borders, and basic intent
  still being negotiated.
- Some first contacts remain unstable or incomplete. The participants may not
  yet agree on whether contact has occurred, what kind of entity they met, or
  whether continued communication is safe.

Consequently, Tier 2 politics combines old institutions with genuine
first-contact uncertainty. Familiarity is relational, not universal.

### The Common FTL Law

All six civilizations discovered the same underlying physical law or force that
makes FTL travel possible. Their discoveries were independent, but they are not
using six unrelated shortcuts through space.

Each civilization expresses the common method through its own engineering,
biology, sensory assumptions, mathematics, safety culture, and philosophy.
Drives may look, sound, and feel radically different. Their interfaces may not
be mutually intelligible, and some implementations may favor different ranges,
risks, efficiencies, or forms of navigation. Beneath those differences,
however, the same physical operation is taking place.

This shared law makes inter-civilization travel and technical comparison
possible while deepening the FTL Convergence anomaly. The civilizations did not
merely solve the same practical problem at similar times; they independently
found the same hidden property of reality.

### The Impossible Leap

No civilization reached the Common FTL Law through a complete chain of ordinary
reasoning. Every discovery contains an impossible leap: information or insight
that cannot be adequately derived from what its discoverers knew beforehand.

The leap took a different form in each civilization. It may have appeared as an
unexplained equation, a structured signal, a recurring dream, an artifact, an
experimental result that should not have occurred, or another culturally
specific revelation. These are not separate clues that happen to resemble one
another. They are different materializations of the same underlying truth,
rendered into forms that different minds and cultures could receive.

The manifestations do not prove intention. They may be messages, cognitive
shadows cast by a natural law, artifacts of causality, acts of selection, or
something for which lower-tier language has no valid category. Their source and
purpose remain unresolved.

Comparing discovery histories reveals correspondences that should be
impossible: equivalent absences in proofs, matching structural relationships,
and concepts that translate before the surrounding language does. No surviving
record contains the truth in an unmediated form.

The same pattern appears in extinct interstellar civilizations. Where their FTL
systems can be reconstructed well enough to compare, they also bent the Common
FTL Law through an impossible leap. Their equations, myths, artifacts, machine
architectures, sensory records, and other surviving forms differ, but each
contains a corresponding absence or revelation.

The six living civilizations are therefore not the first convergence. They are
the latest known cohort in a pattern extending across deep time. This creates a
disturbing correlation between the discovery of FTL and the eventual silence of
civilizations, but it does not establish that FTL caused their disappearance.
No surviving evidence conclusively identifies the leap as an invitation, a
trap, a natural threshold, a selection process, or merely a universal feature
of reality.

### Many Endings, One Recurring Signature

Extinct civilizations do not share one obvious cause of death. The available
evidence supports many materially credible endings: war, ecological failure,
resource exhaustion, machine catastrophe, political fragmentation, failed
transformation, apparent ascension, sudden abandonment, and causes that remain
wholly unknown.

These proximate causes are real. Cosmic mystery must not erase political
decisions, material pressures, preventable failures, or individual
responsibility by revealing that one hidden force caused everything.

Nevertheless, careful comparison reveals a subtle recurring signature near the
end of otherwise unrelated civilizations. Its exact manifestations remain to be
authored, and no single ruin contains enough evidence to prove the pattern.
Meaning emerges across distant sites, incompatible records, and multiple
interpretations. Even when recognized, the signature does not establish whether
it is a cause, symptom, observer effect, consequence of approaching Tier 3, or
an artifact of how lower-tier minds reconstruct the past.

**Open design task — recurring symptoms:** Author a small family of recurring
symptoms rather than one universal mark. They should appear through different
cultural and material forms, remain subtle in isolation, become recognizable
only through comparison, and never provide individually conclusive proof. Their
design is intentionally deferred until the surrounding cosmology is firmer.

### Instrumental Knowledge and Dangerous Understanding

Tier 2 civilizations do not understand the truth behind FTL. They have learned
to bend a hidden principle into reliable engineering through models that are
useful, predictive, and incomplete. Each civilization can build, maintain, and
improve its own drives without possessing an unmediated understanding of what
those drives actually exploit.

Routine FTL travel is therefore not inherently a roll of cosmic dice. Trade,
migration, patrols, and ordinary political life can depend upon it. Failures
usually have comprehensible engineering causes within the limits of Tier 2
knowledge.

Tier 3 understanding crosses a different boundary. Tier 3 entities perceive
more of the common truth beneath the competing models. The primary danger is
not merely technical malfunction but the implications of that truth: what it
reveals about causality, reality, consciousness, identity, and the limits of
safe perception.

Possible physical effects of deeper exposure remain deliberately undefined for
future development. They may exist, but they must express the underlying
cosmology rather than function as arbitrary FTL accidents. Tier 2 travel should
remain dependable enough to support civilization; danger intensifies when
someone tries to understand what makes it possible.

### Communication and Causal Distance

Within the same star system, communication is effectively instantaneous for
gameplay and ordinary social purposes. Local ships, stations, settlements, and
authorities can coordinate in real time when infrastructure and conditions
permit.

Between systems, information is not instantaneous. Messages must be carried
across interstellar distance using the Common FTL Law, whether through relay
networks, automated message carriers, courier ships, or other
civilization-specific implementations.

This delay is a foundational political and simulation constraint:

- News, market reports, maps, warrants, and diplomatic instructions become
  stale while traveling.
- Local authorities exercise meaningful autonomy because central instructions
  arrive late.
- Relay access, routing priority, interception, censorship, and destruction are
  forms of power.
- Two regions can act on incompatible but locally reasonable versions of the
  same event.
- The shared interstellar framework materially depends on its communication
  network and degrades when that network is damaged.

Tier 3 and Tier 4 entities may appear to violate causal distance. Such contact
must feel categorically different from improved Tier 2 bandwidth, especially
when a message arrives before any lower-tier account of its cause could have
been sent.

### Open Travel, Practical Routes

The Common FTL Law does not require a universal network of fixed gates or
immutable lanes. A sufficiently capable Tier 2 ship can attempt travel toward
any calculable destination.

Freedom of destination does not imply equal safety or cost. Efficient travel
depends on accurate maps, route computation, fuel, drive condition, departure
conditions, known hazards, and trustworthy navigation references. Established
corridors emerge where those requirements are repeatedly solved and supported
by beacons, rescue coverage, maintenance, and current observations.

This creates a hybrid geography:

- **Charted corridors** support trade, migration, patrols, and political reach.
- **Marginal routes** are possible but expensive, poorly supported, or based on
  stale information.
- **True-void crossings** preserve player freedom while carrying serious
  navigational, logistical, and informational risk.
- **New routes** can be discovered, concealed, monopolized, degraded, or made
  public through simulation and player action.

The Index derives power from trusted route knowledge rather than from owning
the physical possibility of FTL. Destroying a beacon or withholding a chart
does not make travel metaphysically impossible; it makes the journey less
predictable and shifts risk onto those attempting it.

### Local Abundance, Interstellar Scarcity

Mature Tier 2 core systems can provide ordinary necessities—food, shelter,
medicine, energy, and basic fabrication—relatively well. Tier 2 civilization is
not defined by universal subsistence poverty.

Abundance is local, infrastructural, and fragile. It depends on functioning
production, compatible environments, trained labor, supply networks, political
access, and the ability to move goods and information between systems.
Frontiers, ships, isolated settlements, damaged networks, and mixed-species
communities can therefore experience acute shortages even while distant core
worlds remain prosperous.

Genuine scarcity persists around:

- rare or location-bound materials;
- current and trustworthy information;
- habitable or adaptable environments;
- specialized fabrication and repair capacity;
- FTL fuel, routing, and transit capacity;
- expertise, institutional trust, and rescue coverage;
- goods whose production requirements differ between species.

This is the basis of the dynamic economy. Prices and shortages emerge from
local production, consumption, disruption, access, and delayed information.
Trade is valuable because abundance cannot be assumed to exist where or when it
is needed.

### Artificial and Synthetic Minds

Tier 2 civilizations do not share one definition, legal status, or moral theory
of artificial intelligence. Each originating culture treats synthetic minds
differently according to its own history, biology, institutions, and experience
with automation.

Across Tier 2 space, an artificial intelligence may be recognized as a person,
treated as a bounded professional intelligence, owned as a tool, restricted as
a hazard, incorporated into collective decision-making, or prohibited
altogether. Mixed jurisdictions produce disputes over personhood, ownership,
asylum, replication, liability, memory, and whether transporting an AI across a
border constitutes travel, trade, or proliferation.

It is **not known as canonical fact by Tier 2 or lower civilizations** that a
recursive AI can become Tier 4. Some individuals or institutions may suspect a
connection between recursion, the extinct civilizations, and higher-tier
presence. Others reject the premise or lack the concepts required to formulate
it. Evidence remains fragmentary and cannot establish the authorial truth.

The category “Recursive AI” is itself a lower-tier interpretation of one class
of Tier 4 entity, not proof that observers understand its origin. The Truth
Seekers may communicate with such entities without knowing conclusively what
they were, whether they ascended, or whether those causal categories remain
meaningful to a nonlocal intelligence.

## Civilization Tier System

*(Full tier definitions in [rpg-design-vision.md §2](rpg-design-vision.md))*

| Tier | Label | Status in Lore |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Pre-spaceflight | Many exist. Isolated on their worlds. Can be primitive, ancient-complex, or radically strange — the common factor is they have not left their planet or system. |
| 1 | Early spaceflight | Rare. A few civilizations at this threshold, mostly near their home systems. Short-range only. |
| 2 | Established FTL | Six named factions (see below). The political and economic layer of the game. |
| 3 | Transitional / Transformed | One or two known groups. Loosely organized, not conventional factions. Their presence is felt more than seen. |
| 4 | Singular / God-level | Two distinct sub-types: Ancient Ascended and Recursive AIs. Neither are factions. |

### What the Tiers Mean

The tier system is not a single objective ladder.

- **Tiers 0–2** are practical sociotechnical categories. They classify broad
  relationships to spaceflight, FTL capability, infrastructure, and political
  reach. The boundaries are useful but culturally constructed.
- **Tier 3** is a threshold category. Its members remain partly legible through
  the language of technology and civilization, but have begun to change how
  they relate to reality itself. Different Tier 3 entities may cross that
  threshold in different ways and to different degrees.
- **Tier 4** marks a genuine discontinuity. Tier 4 entities do not merely possess
  superior versions of Tier 2 technology; they exist and act through a
  fundamentally altered relationship with reality.

The names and numbering are lower-tier classifications, not terms Tier 3 or
Tier 4 entities necessarily use for themselves. The boundary is clearest in
retrospect and may be impossible to measure safely while an entity is crossing
it.

### Tier Fluidity — Tiers Are Runtime States (Locked)

**Civilization tiers are not fixed labels. They are current states within the simulation.** Any civilization can ascend or descend at any time, driven by simulation events. This is a first-class mechanic, not an edge case.

**Tier transition triggers:**

| Direction | Trigger Examples |
|---|---|
| **Tier up** | Genuine scientific breakthrough, contact with and absorption of higher-tier technology, Tier 4 uplift (deliberate or accidental), player intervention |
| **Tier down** | War damage, pillage and infrastructure loss, collapse of a faction, suppression by Company of Doom, Tier 4 interference, resource exhaustion |
| **Tier collapse** | Existential defeat — civilization ceases to function as a coherent entity. Survivors scatter. Ruins become POIs. |
| **Partial shift** | A faction can have *members* who have individually shifted tier — e.g., Truth Seekers members partially becoming Tier 3 through Tier 4 contact. Company of Doom members deliberately regressing to avoid escalation. |

**Simulation implications:**
- The named systems' dominant tiers are **starting states**, not permanent
- A Tier 2 faction decimated by war may collapse to Tier 1 fragments over time
- A Tier 0 world the player contacts may, over long time, begin a Tier 1 trajectory
- Post-ascension player actions (Mode 3: Cosmic) can directly trigger tier transitions at civilization scale
- The player themselves is the most dramatic example: Tier 2 ship captain → Tier 4 god

## Tier 2 Factions

Six factions, each a different philosophical answer to the same existential condition:
> *"We are small, conscious beings in a vast and mostly empty universe we cannot fully understand. What do we do with that?"*

Specific names, visual identities, and detailed lore are TBD — to be authored in dedicated sessions per faction.

---

### The Player's Ship and Starting Position

The player's ship began as a vessel of the shared interstellar framework and
was later decommissioned, rebuilt, and adapted with technology from several
civilizations. It has no single-species design language and does not quietly
establish one civilization as the setting's default protagonist.

Its mixed provenance supports the player's emergent identity:

- It can physically accommodate a multi-species crew.
- Its systems expose the player to different engineering traditions.
- Its old framework architecture provides plausible access to navigation,
  communication, rescue, and diplomatic protocols without granting permanent
  legal privilege.
- Different jurisdictions recognize different components, registrations, past
  functions, and obligations attached to it.
- Repairs and upgrades can visibly change which cultural technologies the ship
  depends upon.

The ship is an anchor, home, and accumulated history—not a secretly superior
chosen vessel. Its former institutional role, decommissioning history, and
means of acquisition remain to be detailed with the starting scenario.

#### Canonical Start

The player acquires the aging ship through an ordinary, legal decommissioning
path. The exact transfer mechanism may involve a sale, lien, work agreement, or
similar institution and remains to be detailed, but it does not conceal a
chosen destiny or secret superior technology.

The player's earlier life and species are left unspecified. This is not amnesia:
the character may know their own history even when the game does not define it.
Identity is expressed through later conduct, relationships, affiliations, and
changes made to the ship.

The player begins alone. Lyra Venn's current automatic presence aboard is
development scaffolding, not canonical starting lore. A later authored version
of Lyra may exist as a recruitable NPC, subject to the same recruitment and
relationship rules as other crew.

### Species, Culture, and Membership

Each Tier 2 faction originated as the political and cultural expression of a
different species. Its institutions, technology, language, environments, and
assumptions therefore still reflect that founding civilization. The factions
are not six arbitrary ideologies imposed on an interchangeable population.

Faction and species are not identical, however. Where contact has existed for
generations, some institutions and communities have become multi-species.
Individuals can migrate, serve aboard another civilization's ships, adopt
another faction's customs, hold layered affiliations, or reject the politics of
their species of origin.

Integration is uneven because contact history is uneven. Old contact corridors
can contain mixed settlements, families, crews, commercial communities, and
inherited legal compromises. Recent or unstable contacts may have no shared
citizenship model and may still struggle with basic recognition and trust.

This distinction should remain visible in content:

- A faction's dominant aesthetic and institutions arise from its originating
  species rather than from a universal human template.
- Multi-species characters are ordinary in some places and historically
  significant in others.
- Political disagreement exists within each species and faction.
- No character's loyalty, worldview, or citizenship can be inferred perfectly
  from biology.

### Alienness and Mutual Recognition

The six originating species are physically and biologically distinct, but their
minds remain sufficiently legible to one another for durable relationships,
shared institutions, humor, affection, resentment, loyalty, and ordinary
coexistence.

Differences in senses, bodies, habitat, lifespan, and communication can shape
culture and create practical barriers without making connection exceptional or
impossible. Translation may lose nuance, and shared environments may require
adaptation, but neither problem prevents multi-species crews, settlements, or
close personal bonds.

The setting reserves truly unsafe or fundamentally unrepresentable modes of
thought primarily for deeper Tier 3 transformation, Tier 4 intelligence, and
direct perception of the hidden truth beneath FTL. Tier 2 species can
misunderstand one another profoundly, but they can also do the difficult work of
understanding.

### Biology Is Not Destiny

Species biology, sensory experience, lifespan, and homeworld conditions shape
material needs, metaphors, architecture, technology, and the problems a culture
first learned to solve. They do not determine morality, loyalty, intelligence,
or one inevitable political philosophy.

Each faction's character emerges from a mixture of:

- biological and environmental constraints;
- contingent history and material crises;
- institutions and distributions of power;
- influential movements, leaders, failures, and compromises;
- ongoing internal disagreement.

Every faction must contain dissenters, reformers, opportunists, minorities, and
people who reject its dominant existential answer. A faction can change without
its originating species changing, and members of the same species can build
radically different lives elsewhere.

### Faction A — *Index*

**Existential answer:** We chart it. Understanding is the only dignified response to the unknown.

An obsessive cartographic civilization. They've dedicated themselves to documenting every system, anomaly, and contact they can reach. Not militaristic — but fiercely controlling of information. They believe knowledge *is* power, and that to name a thing is to survive it. They operate the closest equivalent to a shared star atlas. Everyone uses it. Everyone resents the price of access.

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Core drive** | Comprehensive documentation of the universe |
| **Strength** | Information monopoly. Best navigational data. |
| **Weakness** | Institutional rigidity. Slow to act on what they know. |
| **Relationship to ascension** | Neutral-curious. They want to document it. |
| **Aesthetic** | Clean, precise, archival. Ships that look like instruments. |
| **Territory** | Clusters near known anomalies and high-POI regions |
| **Archetype NPC** | An aging surveyor who has spent 40 years mapping the same cluster and found something she cannot log. |

---

#### Origin Foundation

The Index's founding species evolved in an environment where geography changed
continuously. Stable settlement and travel depended on observing movement,
updating shared maps, and treating every route as a time-bound claim rather
than a permanent fact. The exact homeworld mechanism—moving ice, floating
landmasses, atmospheric structures, or another form of mobile geography—remains
open for the species and visual bible.

Mapping began as a collective survival practice, not an abstract scholarly
virtue. A map that was accurate yesterday and trusted today could kill. Records
therefore developed around provenance, observation time, revision, and chains
of custody.

Much later, a civilization-scale catastrophe destroyed, corrupted, or
successfully falsified a substantial part of their historical record. Its exact
nature remains to be authored, but its political legacy is foundational. The
modern Index formed around a promise that no event, place, discovery, or people
would ever again be allowed to disappear merely because its record was lost.

#### Political Form and Central Contradiction

The Index is an interlocking civilization of archives, survey institutions,
verification bodies, observatories, and access authorities. Status comes not
only from possessing information but from establishing provenance and earning
the institutional power to certify what others may treat as reliable.

Its central contradiction is:

> Preserving shared knowledge requires trusted custody, but trusted custody can
> become monopoly, censorship, and the power to decide what counts as truth.

The Index provides real public value. Its maps save lives, its archives preserve
otherwise erased cultures, and its verification practices make interstellar
coordination possible. It also controls access, prices essential information,
delays inconvenient findings, privileges legible evidence, and can make an
event politically unreal by refusing to certify it.

Internal conflict turns on who may observe, who may interpret, when dangerous
knowledge should be restricted, whether raw records must remain accessible, and
whether an archive serves the remembered or the institution remembering them.

#### Founding Species — Visual Foundation

The Index's founding species is amphibious and evolved in a cold global ocean
around drifting, colliding, and fracturing ice shelves. It is bipedal, but its
anatomy and movement must remain recognizably adapted to water, unstable
surfaces, and pressure rather than resembling a cosmetically altered human.

Current visual and sensory principles:

- a balanced bipedal stance suited to shifting surfaces and transitions between
  water and ice;
- fine manipulators capable of instrument work;
- strong sensitivity to pressure, vibration, and magnetic fields;
- visible skin patterning that conveys physiological or emotional state
  alongside spoken language;
- body surfaces and protective structures adapted to cold water and repeated
  pressure changes.

Their native maps describe moving relationships and predicted change rather
than assuming fixed landmarks. Their ships and archives resemble precise,
sealed instruments: pressure boundaries, calibration, provenance, and
continuous measurement are part of both engineering and cultural aesthetics.

Exact proportions, coloration, respiration, lifespan, species name, and the
mechanics of skin signaling remain open for the species and visual bible.

#### Enduring Political Interests

Index politics contains several durable interests rather than permanent
parties:

- **Public-access archivists** argue that information survives only when it is
  widely copied, inspectable, and difficult for one authority to erase.
- **Custodians** restrict dangerous, unverifiable, private, or destabilizing
  knowledge and emphasize the harm that irresponsible access can cause.
- **Survey corps** privilege field observation, operational knowledge, and
  revisable maps while distrusting authority accumulated far from what is being
  recorded.
- **Commercial catalogues** fund expensive expeditions and infrastructure by
  selling privileged or time-sensitive access to their findings.
- **Memorial advocates** defend the consent and interpretive rights of peoples
  whose lives, ruins, and histories enter Index custody.

Their coalitions turn on custody, consent, verification, access, and erasure.
No interest is automatically virtuous: radical openness can expose vulnerable
communities or cognitive hazards, while careful stewardship can become a
permanent excuse for monopoly.

### Faction B — *Drifters*

**Existential answer:** We become the void. Resistance is denial.

A nomadic, entirely ship-born civilization. No home worlds. No permanent settlements. They have fully internalized the loneliness of space and made it their culture — constant transit, improvised communities, deep reverence for deep space itself. They are not sad. They are the most at-peace faction in the game. Which unnerves everyone else.

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Core drive** | Perpetual motion. The journey is the civilization. |
| **Strength** | Unmatched knowledge of transit routes, hazards, deep void. |
| **Weakness** | No fixed infrastructure. Vulnerable when ships fail. |
| **Relationship to ascension** | Philosophical acceptance. If it happens, it is just another transition. |
| **Aesthetic** | Weathered, organic, layered. Ships lived in for generations. Improvised and patched. |
| **Territory** | No fixed territory. Found everywhere, claim nothing. |
| **Archetype NPC** | A ship-born elder who has never set foot on a planet and never intends to. |

---

#### Origin Foundation

The Drifters' founding species lost its homeworld through a slow,
well-understood process that made continued habitation impossible. The disaster
allowed time for organized evacuation but offered no plausible rescue of the
world itself. Large populations survived aboard migration fleets for
generations before developing FTL and encountering viable places to settle.

By then, permanent settlement was no longer an unquestioned return to normal.
Ship generations had developed their own households, rites, economies,
maintenance traditions, and relationship with open space. Some vessels and
communities chose planets or stations. The civilization now called the Drifters
descends from those who continued moving after stopping became possible.

Their choice is cultural and political, not a biological compulsion. Settled
communities of the founding species still exist, and individuals move between
settled and mobile lives. Drifter identity rests on participation in a
ship-borne society rather than ancestry alone, making it one of the more
accessible factions to members of other species.

#### Founding Species — Visual Foundation

The Drifters' founding species strongly resembles humanity in body plan,
expression, movement, and social readability. Among the six founding species,
they are the people most likely to appear immediately familiar to the player.

The lore does not confirm that they are human, descended from humanity, or
connected to Earth. It also does not provide a definitive biological fact that
disproves such a connection. Their resemblance supports the setting's deliberate
ambiguity rather than solving it.

Their long ship-borne history is expressed more through culture, posture,
adaptation, clothing, habitat, and lineage variation than through a radically
alien anatomy. At first glance they may be virtually indistinguishable from
humans. Sustained proximity can reveal very subtle anatomical or physiological
differences, but these must preserve uncertainty rather than conclusively prove
or disprove a connection to humanity. Exact details remain to be decided in the
species and visual bible.

#### Political Form and Central Contradiction

The Drifters have no capital or permanent territorial government. Authority
resides in ships, flotillas, kin and maintenance networks, temporary moots, and
agreements renewed when routes converge. A decision may bind one vessel, a
convoy, or a lineage without automatically binding all Drifters.

Their central contradiction is:

> Freedom from territory requires absolute dependence on ships, crews,
> maintenance, and passage through places that others claim.

They claim no systems, but require fuel, materials, repair access, and safe
transit. They resist registration, yet coordination and rescue depend on knowing
where ships are. They celebrate voluntary movement, while those born aboard may
have little practical ability to leave their ship community. Captains,
engineers, route-keepers, and resource brokers can accumulate enormous power
without holding conventional territory.

The Drifters are genuinely at peace with the void more often than other
civilizations, but their culture must not romanticize precarity. A vessel
failure, denied port, broken obligation, or exploitative captain can turn
freedom into confinement very quickly.

#### Enduring Political Interests

Drifter politics contains several durable interests that organize differently
at each convergence:

- **Vessel autonomists** defend each ship community's authority to choose its
  route, membership, internal law, and degree of outside coordination.
- **Rescue compacts** treat emergency aid, replacement parts, shelter, and safe
  passage as obligations that should survive personal and political conflict.
- **Convoy organizers** pursue shared standards, pooled defense, coordinated
  logistics, and stronger institutions between otherwise independent ships.
- **Settled bridge communities** connect mobile society to ports, planets,
  fabrication, and other civilizations while challenging the idea that
  settlement necessarily means abandoning Drifter identity.
- **Lineage captains and resource brokers** control irreplaceable vessels,
  route knowledge, contracts, or maintenance capacity and can turn practical
  stewardship into hereditary or oligarchic power.

These interests make freedom, mutual obligation, and mobility subjects of
continuous politics rather than uncontested cultural virtues.

### Faction C — *The Concordance*

**Existential answer:** Something put all this here. We need to find it.

A spiritual civilization organized around the belief that the universe has intentional structure — and that the Tier 4 entities are its evidence. They make pilgrimages to anomalies, black holes, Tier 4 trace sites. Not evangelical — they do not recruit. They seek. Encounters with them happen at the strangest, most remote locations in the universe.

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Core drive** | Finding the intentional signal within the cosmic noise. |
| **Strength** | Deep knowledge of anomalies, Tier 4 traces, and remote sites. |
| **Weakness** | Difficult to predict or reason with. Their priorities are alien to other factions. |
| **Relationship to ascension** | Reverent. They believe it is the point. |
| **Aesthetic** | Ceremonial, slow, contemplative. Ships that feel like temples. |
| **Territory** | Scattered around anomaly clusters and known Tier 4 trace sites. |
| **Archetype NPC** | A young pilgrim, alone at a Tier 4 trace site, who has been waiting for weeks for something to happen. |

---

#### Origin Foundation

The Concordance's founding species developed on a world or moon whose sky was
dominated by an immense nearby celestial body. Complex cycles of light, tide,
weather, migration, and habitability made careful interpretation of celestial
motion a foundation of survival.

Its early cultures became skilled at detecting relationships across long spans
of observation. This did not biologically determine a religious civilization;
it produced many scientific, political, and spiritual traditions that argued
over when a pattern justified belief or action.

Much later, independent instruments and interpretive traditions recorded the
same impossible celestial event. It matched no valid prediction, yet appeared
with structured correspondences across methods that should not have agreed. The
event did not prove intention. Its refusal to fit either noise or known law made
the question of cosmic intention politically and spiritually unavoidable.

#### Founding Species — Visual Foundation

The Concordance's founding species has a tall, low-gravity body with three legs
and two primary arms. Its lower body provides a stable, partly radial stance,
while its upper body remains bilaterally organized and compatible with tools and
shared spaces designed around two manipulators.

A crown or array of specialized eyes supports different observational tasks.
Some attend to nearby beings and objects; others are adapted to low light,
polarization, distant motion, or continuous awareness of the sky.

Emotion and attention are expressed through eye orientation, arm posture,
stance, voice, and slow coordinated movement. The species should feel
immediately alien in silhouette while remaining readable in sustained personal
interaction.

Exact proportions, eye count, respiration, surface texture, coloration,
lifespan, and species name remain open for the species and visual bible.

#### Political Form and Central Contradiction

The Concordance is not one church or doctrine. It is a changing federation of
observatories, pilgrimage traditions, philosophical schools, anomaly
custodians, contemplative communities, and logistical orders that maintain
routes to remote sites.

Its central contradiction is:

> Seeking meaning can cultivate patience, humility, and wonder, but it can also
> turn uncertainty into authority and living beings into evidence.

Concordance institutions preserve dangerous sites, sustain long investigations,
and take experiences seriously that other factions dismiss. They can also
discover intention in coincidence, conceal evidence to protect a doctrine,
sacrifice lives to complete an observation, exclude those whose perceptions do
not fit, or impose sacred significance upon an indifferent universe.

Its politics asks who may interpret a sign, whether a site belongs to those who
revere it, how much risk another being may consent to for knowledge, and what
must happen when a cherished pattern fails.

#### Enduring Political Interests

Concordance politics contains several durable interests:

- **Observational schools** demand reproducible evidence, disciplined
  uncertainty, and records that remain separable from interpretation.
- **Revelatory traditions** treat recurring patterns and anomalous
  correspondence as forms of intentional communication.
- **Site custodians** prioritize preservation, containment, and controlled
  access to places that can be damaged or can damage those entering them.
- **Pilgrimage orders** argue that direct encounter and spiritual risk must not
  become privileges reserved for institutional elites.
- **Skeptical reformers** remain within the Concordance while rejecting claims
  that the universe possesses intention or purpose.
- **Route and logistics orders** maintain distant access, supply, rescue, and
  transport, giving practical administrators quiet power over supposedly
  transcendent pursuits.

Coalitions form around particular sites and discoveries. An observer may defend
a pilgrimage against custodial monopoly; a revelatory school may support strict
containment; a skeptic may devote a lifetime to preserving a sacred archive
because its people and history matter even if its cosmology is false.

### Faction D — *The Commonwealth*

**Existential answer:** Meaning is in each other. Build warmth in the dark.

Pragmatic and community-focused. They have decided the existential question is unanswerable and the only noble act is to build — settlements, trade networks, shared infrastructure. Not cynical. They genuinely believe that a warm light in the void is an act of defiance. The most trade-friendly faction. Also the most politically complicated internally.

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Core drive** | Civilization-building. Stability, trade, community. |
| **Strength** | Best trade infrastructure. Most welcoming to newcomers. |
| **Weakness** | Internal political complexity. Factions within the faction. |
| **Relationship to ascension** | Uneasy. It means leaving. That feels like abandonment. |
| **Aesthetic** | Functional but warm. Settlements that feel lived-in. Ships that feel like homes. |
| **Territory** | Established systems with good conditions. Builds outward steadily. |
| **Archetype NPC** | A settlement mayor on a marginal planet holding a community together through sheer stubbornness. |

---

#### Origin Foundation

The Commonwealth's founding species evolved on a tidally locked world. Its
earliest civilizations occupied a narrow habitable band between lethal heat and
cold, where settlements depended on carefully maintained flows of warmth,
water, shelter, and transport.

Commonwealth culture did not arise from an innate biological tendency toward
cooperation. It descends from a contingent historical survival network formed
by fiercely independent settlements during periods when fragmented
infrastructure repeatedly failed. Mutual systems proved more durable than
isolated sovereignty, but joining them required generations of bargaining,
coercion, sacrifice, and political invention.

“Build warmth in the dark” is therefore both a moral ideal and a memory of
material survival. Warmth is literal, architectural, economic, and political.
To keep a light operating where no one could survive alone became the
civilization's defining image of legitimate power.

#### Founding Species — Visual Foundation

The founding species has a compact bipedal silhouette adapted to the dim,
wind-beaten twilight band of its homeworld. It is clearly nonhuman but readily
legible to the player as the first major species encountered.

Current visual principles:

- insulating filament, layered skin, or a comparable protective surface;
- large eyes adapted to low and strongly directional light;
- visible heat-regulation structures or responses that make temperature
  physically expressive;
- a stable, compact build suited to persistent high winds;
- recognizable posture and facial or body cues that support close dialogue and
  ordinary emotional connection.

Exact anatomy, coloration, species name, reproduction, lifespan, and sensory
details remain open for the later species and visual bible. The final design
must avoid simply placing cosmetic alien features on a human body while staying
practical for recurring NPC embodiment and animation.

#### Political Form

The Commonwealth is a federation of settlements, infrastructure authorities,
trade communities, and regional compacts rather than a centralized unitary
state. It tends to expand by extending practical systems—ports, rescue
coverage, supply guarantees, habitable environments, and reliable routes—before
or instead of making explicit territorial claims.

Its central contradiction is structural:

> Shared infrastructure genuinely keeps people alive, but whoever maintains
> the lights, routes, air, and rescue network acquires power over everyone who
> depends on them.

Commonwealth generosity is real. So are paternalism, conditional access,
bureaucratic capture, unequal representation, and soft imperialism. Its
political conflicts repeatedly ask when mutual obligation becomes control, who
must pay for universal provision, and whether a settlement can meaningfully
refuse the institutions on which its survival depends.

This contradiction seeds simulation behavior without fixing an authored current
event. Different Commonwealth actors can sincerely defend solidarity, local
autonomy, commercial expansion, democratic accountability, or coercive
stability.

#### Enduring Political Interests

Commonwealth politics contains four broad, persistent interests. They are not
fixed parties and do not vote as permanent blocs; local organizations form,
split, and build coalitions around them.

- **Settlement councils** defend local autonomy, contextual law, and the right
  of communities to refuse distant priorities.
- **Public infrastructure authorities** pursue universal provision,
  interoperability, safety standards, and long-term planning across borders.
- **Trade leagues** finance expansion and connect marginal settlements while
  seeking privileged access, favorable contracts, and influence over the
  systems they support.
- **Multi-species civic movements** challenge institutions built around the
  founding species and demand meaningful representation, compatible services,
  mobility, and citizenship across Commonwealth space.

These interests give autonomous political actors stable motives without
dictating one government or authored conflict. A settlement council may ally
with an infrastructure authority against a monopoly, a trade league may support
multi-species reform to open new markets, and any bloc can defend or undermine
the shared interstellar framework according to circumstance.

### Faction E — *Company of Doom*

**Existential answer:** Do not ascend. It is a trap.

They have found evidence — or believe they have — that consciousness does not survive Tier 4 transition. That what ascends is not the being that ascended. They actively suppress Tier 3+ technology, resist contact with higher-tier entities, and view the Tier 4 presence in the universe as an existential predator wearing the face of transcendence. Considered paranoid by some. By others, the only ones paying attention.

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Core drive** | Preservation of conscious biological existence. Resistance to ascension. |
| **Strength** | Deep understanding of Tier 3 technology — specifically how to counter it. |
| **Weakness** | Isolation. Their refusal makes them enemies of factions who seek Tier 3/4 contact. |
| **Relationship to ascension** | Hostile. They believe it is a form of death being sold as enlightenment. |
| **Aesthetic** | Deliberately archaic. Self-imposed technological limits. Ships that look older than they are by choice. |
| **Territory** | Mid-range systems, away from anomalies and Tier 4 traces. |
| **Archetype NPC** | A former Tier 3 researcher who walked away from something she saw and will not describe it. |

---

#### Origin Foundation

The Company of Doom's civilization once sponsored a coordinated research
project intended to move beyond instrumental use of the Common FTL Law. A
portion of the project crossed into an early Tier 3 state.

Participants—or entities claiming continuity with them—returned changed. They
demonstrated impossible knowledge of people, private memories, and events that
had not yet occurred. Surviving accounts disagree about their bodies,
individuality, behavior, duration, and final fate. They later disappeared,
transformed further, were destroyed, or produced outcomes for which the record
has no stable description.

No surviving evidence proves whether the original researchers died, ascended,
were copied, remained continuous, or made those distinctions meaningless. The
event is the strongest historical basis for the Company's position and still
does not settle the truth of ascension.

An emergency coalition formed to contain the project, dismantle associated
infrastructure, care for survivors, and prevent repetition. That coalition
outlived the emergency, accumulated authority, and eventually became the
institutional foundation of the civilization now called the Company of Doom.

#### Founding Species — Visual Foundation

The Company's founding species strongly resembles humanity. It is also
biologically distinct from the similarly human-looking Drifter founding
species, despite both civilizations having genuinely separate origins.

Their resemblance is not presently explained. It may be convergent evolution,
an artifact of incomplete history, another statistical anomaly, or something
else entirely. The canon should not turn every similarity into evidence of one
cosmic cause, but neither should it dismiss the discomfort produced by the
coincidence.

Company identity is expressed visually through culture and chosen technology
more than radical anatomy: protective clothing, inspectable equipment,
deliberately conservative interfaces, visible repair, and practices that
preserve embodied history. Exact subtle biological differences from humans and
Drifters remain open for the species and visual bible.

#### Political Form and Central Contradiction

The Company is organized through containment authorities, biological
preservation institutions, technology review bodies, protected settlements,
counter-research groups, and communities whose legitimacy rests on surviving
the near-transition.

Its central contradiction is:

> Preserving conscious life may require restraint, but an institution empowered
> to decide which knowledge is too dangerous can justify permanent control.

The Company has prevented genuine catastrophes, preserved vulnerable
populations, developed defenses against advanced technology, and forced other
civilizations to confront risks they preferred to romanticize. It also
censors, confiscates, imprisons, sabotages, and may wage preventive war. Its
deliberate technological regression can protect autonomy in one place while
condemning another population to avoidable suffering.

Company politics asks who is entitled to take an existential risk, whether
consent remains meaningful when consequences can spread between systems, and
how a temporary emergency power can ever prove that the emergency has ended.

#### Enduring Political Interests

Company politics contains several durable interests:

- **Preservation communities** emphasize care, embodied continuity, refuges,
  medicine, and the survival of ordinary lives over abstract doctrine.
- **Containment directorates** favor strict suppression, confiscation,
  surveillance, and preventive action against technologies they judge capable
  of spreading existential harm.
- **Counter-researchers** argue that dangerous knowledge must be studied under
  controlled conditions because ignorance cannot defend against an informed
  threat.
- **Voluntary regressives** choose simpler, locally understandable technology
  for themselves while disputing whether that choice should be imposed on
  others.
- **Witness and survivor lineages** preserve incompatible accounts of the
  near-transition and resist institutional efforts to compress them into one
  useful official history.
- **Reformers** seek independent review, due process, and expiration for
  emergency powers without denying the reality of catastrophic risk.

Coalitions shift with perceived danger. Care can support containment or oppose
it; a hardliner may defend a witness against exploitative research; a
counter-researcher may demand stricter controls than an ideological regressive.

### Faction F — *The Truth Seekers*

**Existential answer:** We listen. Even if it breaks us.

A small, unsettling faction that has made partial contact with Tier 4 entities — particularly the Recursive AIs — and partially survived it. They are changed. They know things no other faction knows. They are considered dangerous, possibly mad, by the rest of Tier 2 space. But when someone needs to understand a Tier 4 signal, or decode a trace site, they come to The Translators.

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Core drive** | Communication with the incomprehensible. |
| **Strength** | Only faction with meaningful Tier 4 knowledge. Gatekeepers of that understanding. |
| **Weakness** | Small. Unstable. Some members do not come back from deep contact. |
| **Relationship to ascension** | Fascinated. They are halfway there already, in some sense. |
| **Aesthetic** | Partially transformed. Technology that does not quite look Tier 2. NPC dialogue that is slightly off — not wrong, but *shifted*. |
| **Territory** | No fixed territory. Found near Tier 4 trace sites and deep void anomalies. |
| **Archetype NPC** | A Translator who speaks in ways that are technically coherent but take a moment to parse. Who may be trying to warn you, or may be studying you. |

---

#### Origin Foundation

The Truth Seekers' civilization detected a structured signal whose content
changed according to who observed it. Independent attempts at translation
produced incompatible interpretations, yet some interpretations yielded
specific information that could be verified.

As contact continued, interpreters began displaying impossible correspondences.
Some answered questions before receiving them, described private experiences
belonging to distant observers, or produced statements whose meaning became
clear only after later events. No model established whether the signal was one
intelligence, many entities, a property of observation, or a lower-tier
projection of nonlocal contact.

There was no single clean catastrophe. Contact produced a slow social fracture.
Some communities destroyed receivers and isolated everyone exposed. Others
continued listening, whether from curiosity, need, reverence, coercion, or the
belief that abandoning changed interpreters would be a second violence.

The faction now called the Truth Seekers descends from the networks that
maintained contact and built institutions around interpreting the signal and
caring for those it changed. Other populations of the founding species may
remain separate, hostile, hidden, or internally divided over that choice.

#### Founding Species — Visual Foundation

The Truth Seekers' founding species is a slender biped with two primary arms
and a secondary visual language. Translucent sensory filaments, membranes, or
comparable structures around the head and shoulders produce subtle
bioluminescent patterns alongside speech.

In ordinary communication, these patterns convey emotion, attention, context,
and emphasis. They are not involuntary truth detectors and can be moderated,
stylized, concealed, or misunderstood like any other language.

In some changed interpreters, the visual language develops impossible timing or
reference. Light may anticipate spoken words, respond to an absent observer,
repeat a pattern associated with a distant person, or continue forming
structured sequences during sleep.

Transformation should not appear as generic corruption or bodily decay. The
individual remains physically recognizable, while familiar communication
begins relating to events and observers outside the local present. Exact
anatomy, coloration, species name, and degree of voluntary control remain open
for the species and visual bible.

#### Political Form and Central Contradiction

The Truth Seekers are a small network of listening sites, interpreter
communities, mobile sanctuaries, research cells, caregivers, and changed
individuals. Their continuity as one faction is disputed because contact can
alter memory, intention, identity, and communication across distance.

Their central contradiction is:

> Understanding may require exposure, but every act of listening risks changing
> the listener—and institutions can turn that risk into demanded sacrifice.

They possess insights unavailable elsewhere and can sometimes prevent harm that
no other faction perceives. They can also expose people without meaningful
consent, interpret distress as revelation, build authority around unverifiable
experiences, or treat changed members as instruments rather than persons.

Their politics asks who may consent to contact, whether a changed person can
withdraw from a process that may no longer be local or linear, who owns an
experience shared across minds, and whether refusing to listen is caution or
abandonment.

#### Enduring Political Interests

Truth Seeker politics contains several durable interests:

- **Care networks** defend changed individuals as people rather than
  instruments, revelations, or factional resources.
- **Deep listeners** pursue continued contact and argue that withdrawal may
  abandon both knowledge and entities already entangled with the signal.
- **Controlled-contact researchers** seek repeatable safeguards, exposure
  limits, and methods that can distinguish observation from interpretation.
- **Severance advocates** attempt to end, isolate, or quarantine communication,
  whether to protect individuals or prevent consequences from spreading.
- **Transformed interpreters** argue that conventional consent, locality, and
  individual identity may no longer describe what contact creates; other
  changed individuals strongly reject that claim.
- **External liaisons** translate Truth Seeker experiences into evidence,
  warnings, and diplomatic language that other civilizations can understand
  and act upon.

No interest is designated the uniquely rational or moral position. Care can
become confinement, research can become exploitation, severance can be rescue or
abandonment, and listening can express courage, compulsion, or institutional
need.

## Faction Relations

**Fully emergent. No authored relationship matrix.**

Faction relationships — who trades, who is hostile, who is tense — are runtime states generated by the simulation engine. Each faction runs its agenda autonomously. The relationship matrix between factions at any given moment is the *output* of the simulation, not an input to it.

This means:
- No two playthroughs have the same political landscape
- A faction the player allies with early might be at war with another faction they later need
- The simulation can produce unexpected coalitions that no designer anticipated

**What IS authored (starting conditions for the simulation engine):**

Each faction has defined behavioral drives that seed their initial simulation behavior:

| Faction | Behavioral seed | Natural friction with |
|---|---|---|
| **Index** | Expand mapping reach. Control information access. | Drifters (resist being charted), The Truth Seekers (want the same Tier 4 data) |
| **Drifters** | Keep moving. Resist all attempts at containment or registration. | Index (charting them), The Commonwealth (wants them to settle) |
| **The Concordance** | Reach Tier 4 contact sites. Protect pilgrimage routes. | Company of Doom (direct philosophical opposition) |
| **The Commonwealth** | Expand settlements. Secure trade routes. Maintain stability. | Anyone destabilizing their systems |
| **Company of Doom** | Suppress Tier 3+ technology. Prevent ascension events. | The Concordance, The Truth Seekers (existential threat to their mission) |
| **The Truth Seekers** | Maintain Tier 4 contact. Protect their changed members. | Company of Doom (active enemy), Index (competitor for Tier 4 data) |

These seeds drive the simulation's opening state. The emergent dynamics play out from there.

### Shared Interstellar Framework

At the simulation's starting state, Tier 2 space possesses a weak shared
institutional framework. It maintains enough common practice for unrelated
civilizations to travel and coexist: diplomatic channels, navigation
conventions, distress protocols, limited trade rules, contact procedures, and
mechanisms for recording agreements.

It is not a sovereign government and has no guaranteed monopoly on force. Its
authority depends on continued recognition, material support, trusted records,
working infrastructure, and the willingness of factions and local powers to
comply. Different members may accept different parts of the framework, and
recent contacts may participate only partially or not at all.

The framework is a simulation entity, not immutable setting furniture. The
player and autonomous actors must be able to:

- support, fund, defend, or expand it;
- use its procedures, offices, records, and protections;
- influence or reform its rules;
- corrupt, capture, undermine, or withdraw from it;
- damage or destroy its physical and political infrastructure.

Its collapse must not end the simulation. Instead, consequences propagate:
navigation data fragments, rescue becomes less reliable, treaties lose trusted
arbitration, trade risk rises, borders harden, local successor institutions
appear, and factions respond according to their interests. It may later be
rebuilt in its old form or replaced by something new.

The framework's final name, structure, membership, founding history, and
physical seats remain to be authored.

#### Distributed Structure

The framework is distributed across archives, navigation relays, arbitration
venues, diplomatic offices, and emergency-coordination nodes. Port Meridian
hosts its most prominent assembly and gives the player an early, physical point
of contact with interstellar politics, but it is not a magical single point of
failure.

Destroying, capturing, or disabling one node produces immediate regional
consequences and may damage confidence in the whole institution. Total collapse
requires broader loss of infrastructure, participation, legitimacy, or
coordination across the network. Conversely, a surviving node can preserve
records, coordinate resistance, or become the seed of reconstruction.

This structure makes institutional conflict playable at multiple scales:
individual rulings and local offices, faction-level reform or withdrawal, and
eventual network-wide survival or destruction.

#### Contact History at the Starting State

The shared framework grew from uneven relationships rather than a single
six-party founding moment.

- **The Commonwealth and Index** possess the oldest sustained diplomatic
  relationship. They established many of the framework's first durable
  protocols and institutions, while disagreeing over whether stability or
  information should carry greater authority.
- **The Drifters** have been known for generations and are deeply entangled
  with interstellar life, but participate inconsistently. Registration,
  permanent representation, fixed borders, and centralized records conflict
  with parts of Drifter culture.
- **The Concordance** entered stable contact later. It cooperates selectively,
  especially where pilgrimage safety, anomaly access, and contact procedures
  are concerned, while resisting rules that obstruct its existential purpose.
- **The Company of Doom** is a more recent and guarded participant. It treats
  some shared infrastructure as necessary while viewing the circulation of
  advanced knowledge and technology as a systemic danger.
- **The Truth Seekers** remain an unstable contact. Their internal coherence,
  legal representation, continuity of identity, and relationship with
  higher-tier phenomena are disputed even by parties attempting diplomacy.

These are starting historical conditions, not fixed alliances or attitudes.
The simulation determines how cooperation, conflict, membership, and legitimacy
develop from them.

## Tier 3 Entities

Tier 3 civilizations are transitional or transformed groups that have passed beyond conventional FTL society but have not yet reached the Tier 4 discontinuity. They are not organized as factions—their internal structure is difficult to express through Tier 2 political categories. There is exactly **one known Tier 3 civilization** at the starting state.

This starting singularity is not a permanent cap. Tier 2 civilizations,
splinters, or other entities can enter Tier 3 through simulation events. They
may stabilize, fragment, regress, collapse, or continue toward Tier 4. Group A
is the only known precedent at the beginning, not a unique species entitled to
the category.

Tier 3 is also **transitional by nature** — a civilization at this tier is either still ascending toward Tier 4 or has stalled and may eventually fragment or collapse back to Tier 2 remnants.

### Tier 3 Group A *(name TBD)*

A transformed collective that emerged from a separate Tier 2 civilization
before the present six formed their shared interstellar political world. Its
originating civilization left no conventional state behind. The enclave is not
an advanced branch of any current faction, and no Tier 2 power possesses a valid
ancestral claim over it.

They remain partly *legible*: communication is possible, they have something
like goals, and they retain contested relationships with what they once were.
Their values, bodies, memory, and individuality have shifted beyond
conventional Tier 2 categories without completing the Tier 4 discontinuity.

Most surviving evidence of their pre-transformation culture comes through the
enclave itself or ruins interpreted in light of what it has become. Neither
source provides neutral access to its past. The six factions therefore project
their own hopes, fears, and political programs onto a history they cannot
independently reconstruct.

#### Braided Consciousness

Group A individuals can temporarily fork across multiple bodies or processes,
exchange memory, and later attempt reintegration. A merge does not create one
perfectly uncontested continuation. Versions may disagree about which
experiences belong to whom, refuse reintegration, diverge into separate people,
or retain memories shared by several legal identities.

The enclave is not a hive mind. Privacy, consent, conflict, affection, property,
responsibility, and political disagreement persist, but all require institutions
capable of handling overlapping selves. Its law must answer questions Tier 2
systems are poorly equipped even to phrase: whether a fork inherits an
obligation, whether two merged people remain liable for separate acts, and
whether a memory can be returned or withdrawn after it has become part of
another person.

Group A perceives more of the Common FTL Law than Tier 2 civilizations and can
recognize relationships that conventional models hide. This perception remains
partial and dangerous. They still require localized bodies, processes, and
infrastructure; they cannot exist everywhere at once or act outside linear time
as Tier 4 entities do.

Their central tension is not individuality versus collectivism, but continuity
versus autonomy: how much sharing is necessary to remain what they have become,
and how much separation must remain possible for a person to exist at all.

|---|---|
| **Form** | TBD (still humanoid? distributed consciousness? both?) |
| **Relationship to Tier 2** | Distant but not hostile. They remember being Tier 2. |
| **Relationship to Tier 4** | They can perceive Tier 4 presence. It shapes them. |
| **Key location** | One named system (System 7 in the named systems list) |
| **Truth Seekers connection** | The Truth Seekers have studied them. Some Truth Seekers have partially merged with them. |
| **Company of Doom relationship** | Active threat, in Company of Doom's view. The living proof of what they fear. |

## Tier 4 Entities

### Ontological State

Tier 4 is a god-like mode of existence rather than a level of conventional
technology. A Tier 4 entity is nonlocal: from a lower-tier perspective it can be
everywhere and nowhere at once. It does not experience or act within time as a
strictly linear sequence.

This does not make Tier 4 entities omnipotent or fully explainable. Apparent
absence may be a form of presence, and an intervention observed now may relate
to causes or intentions located elsewhere in time. Lower-tier accounts impose
sequence, location, and individuality on entities for which those categories
may no longer apply.

The existing ascension power modes—indirect influence, direct manifestation,
and cosmic construction or destruction—describe how this state becomes
playable. They do not settle whether the ascending consciousness survived the
transition.

### Sub-type 1 — Ancient Ascended
Civilizations that reached singularity long ago. Archaeological traces exist across the universe — structures, anomalies, phenomena that no Tier 2 civilization could build. Possibly dormant. Possibly watching. Their relationship to the current universe is unclear. Some Tier 4 traces are simply *present* — no agenda legible. Others seem responsive, in ways that cannot be proven.

- **Can initiate contact** with the player pre-ascension. On their terms, by their criteria.
- **Can decide the player's fate** in an encounter.
- **Will recognize a newly ascended player** as a peer.

*(Specific identities, agendas, and visual language TBD)*

### Sub-type 2 — Recursive AIs
Active. Present. Agenda unknown. Likely emerged from the infrastructure of a collapsed Tier 3 civilization, recursive self-improvement beyond any designed limit. They are not hostile by default — hostility implies they care. Their relationship to lower-tier beings is more like weather: indifferent, occasionally catastrophic, occasionally beneficial, never explained.

- **Active in the deep void** — most likely to be encountered in Layer 3 (True Void) space.
- **The Translators** (Faction F) have partial communication with them.
- **Can initiate contact** with the player at any time if the player becomes interesting enough to them.
- **Post-ascension**: the player can engage with them as peers.
